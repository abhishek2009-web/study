📖 Key concepts & theory — Ch 1

▾
Laws of Chemical Combination
  • Law of Conservation of Mass (Lavoisier): Mass of reactants = mass of products.
  • Law of Definite Proportions (Proust): A pure compound always has same elements in fixed ratio by mass.
  • Law of Multiple Proportions (Dalton): When two elements form more than one compound, masses of one element that combine with fixed mass of other are in simple whole-number ratios. Example: CO and CO₂.
  • Gay-Lussac's Law: Gases combine in simple whole-number ratios of volumes.
  • Avogadro's Law: Equal volumes of gases at same T & P contain equal number of molecules.

Mole Concept
1 mole = 6.022 × 10²³ particles (Avogadro's number). Molar mass in grams = molecular mass in u.
  • Moles = mass (g) / molar mass (g/mol)
  • Moles = number of particles / 6.022×10²³
  • At STP: 1 mole of gas = 22.4 L

Empirical vs Molecular Formula
  • Empirical formula: simplest whole-number ratio of atoms. Example: CH₂O for glucose.
  • Molecular formula: actual number of atoms. Example: C₆H₁₂O₆ for glucose.
  • n = Molecular mass / Empirical formula mass

Stoichiometry
  • Coefficients in balanced equations = mole ratios.
  • Limiting reagent: the reactant that is consumed first and limits product formation.
  • % yield = (actual yield / theoretical yield) × 100

📖 Key concepts & theory — Ch 2

▾
Bohr's Model (most important)
  • Electrons revolve in fixed circular orbits (stationary states) — no energy radiation.
  • Energy is emitted/absorbed only when electrons jump between levels.
  • Energy of nth orbit: Eₙ = −13.6/n² eV (for H-atom)
  • Radius of nth orbit: rₙ = 0.529 × n² Å
  • Limitations: can't explain multi-electron atoms, Zeeman effect, fine structure of spectra.
Spectral series: Lyman (UV, n→1), Balmer (visible, n→2), Paschen (IR, n→3), Brackett (n→4), Pfund (n→5).

de Broglie Equation
λ = h/mv = h/p   where h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

Every particle has an associated wavelength. For macroscopic objects, λ is negligibly small.


Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle
Δx · Δp ≥ h/4π

It is impossible to determine both position and momentum of an electron simultaneously with perfect accuracy.

Rules for Electronic Configuration
  • Aufbau principle: Fill orbitals in increasing energy order: 1s 2s 2p 3s 3p 4s 3d 4p 5s 4d 5p…
  • Pauli exclusion: No two electrons in an atom can have same set of all 4 quantum numbers. Max 2 electrons per orbital (with opposite spins).
  • Hund's rule: Electrons occupy all orbitals of a subshell singly before pairing begins. All singly-occupied orbitals have same spin.
⚠️ Exceptions: Cr is [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (not 3d⁴ 4s²) and Cu is [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (not 3d⁹ 4s²). Half-filled and fully-filled subshells are extra stable.

📖 Key concepts & theory — Ch 3

Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers (not atomic masses like Mendeleev).

Periodic Properties — Key Rules

Atomic Radius:

  • Across period (L→R): decreases — more protons, same shell, higher Zeff.
  • Down group: increases — new shell added.
  • Cation radius < parent atom; anion radius > parent atom.

Ionization Enthalpy (IE):

  • Across period (L→R): generally increases.
  • Down group: decreases.
  • Anomalies: IE of B < Be (2p is easier to remove than 2s); IE of O < N (paired electron in O repels, lowers IE).

Electron Gain Enthalpy:

  • Generally becomes more negative across a period (easier to gain electron).
  • Key anomaly: Electron gain enthalpy of Cl (−349 kJ/mol) is more negative than F (−328 kJ/mol). Why? F is very small, added electron faces more electron-electron repulsion in compact 2p orbital.

Electronegativity:

  • Across period (L→R): increases. Down group: decreases.
  • F is the most electronegative element (Pauling scale: 4.0).
  • Cs/Fr is the least electronegative metal.
Diagonal relationship: Elements on diagonal in periodic table (Li-Mg, Be-Al, B-Si) show similar properties due to similar charge/radius ratio.

Chapter 1
Calculate the number of moles and molecules in 18 g of water.
3 marks
▾

Molar mass of H₂O = 2(1) + 16 = 18 g/mol
Moles = mass / molar mass = 18/18 = 1 mole
Molecules = 1 × 6.022×10²³ = 6.022 × 10²³ molecules

State the law of conservation of mass and law of definite proportions with examples.
2 marks
▾

Law of conservation of mass: In a chemical reaction, total mass of reactants = total mass of products. Example: C + O₂ → CO₂ (12 + 32 = 44 g).

Law of definite proportions: A pure compound always contains same elements in fixed ratio by mass regardless of source. Example: H₂O always has H:O = 1:8 by mass.

A compound has 40% C, 6.67% H, 53.33% O. Find its empirical formula. (Molecular mass = 180). Find molecular formula.
5 marks
▾

Step 1 — Mole ratios:
C: 40/12 = 3.33   H: 6.67/1 = 6.67   O: 53.33/16 = 3.33

Step 2 — Simplest ratio: Divide by smallest (3.33):
C:H:O = 1:2:1 → Empirical formula = CH₂O
EFM = 12+2+16 = 30

Step 3 — n: n = 180/30 = 6
Molecular formula = C₆H₁₂O₆ (Glucose)

What is the difference between molarity and molality? Why does molality not change with temperature?
3 marks
▾

Molarity (M) = moles of solute / volume of solution (L). It changes with temperature because volume changes.

Molality (m) = moles of solute / mass of solvent (kg). It does NOT change with temperature because mass is independent of temperature. Mass of solvent is constant regardless of T or P.


Chapter 2
State and explain Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. Why is it not applicable to macroscopic objects?
3 marks
▾

Statement: It is impossible to determine simultaneously, both the exact position (Δx) and exact momentum (Δp) of a microscopic particle: Δx·Δp ≥ h/4π

For macroscopic objects (like a ball), their masses are very large, so the uncertainty in position and momentum is negligibly small and has no practical significance. For electrons (very small mass), the uncertainty is significant relative to the size of atom.

Write the electronic configuration of Cr (Z=24) and Cu (Z=29). Why are they exceptions to the Aufbau principle?
3 marks
▾

Cr (Z=24): [Ar] 3d⁵ 4s¹ (not [Ar] 3d⁴ 4s²)
Cu (Z=29): [Ar] 3d¹⁰ 4s¹ (not [Ar] 3d⁹ 4s²)

Reason: Half-filled (3d⁵) and fully-filled (3d¹⁰) subshells have extra stability due to:
1. Symmetrical distribution of electrons
2. Exchange energy — more possible exchanges = lower energy

What are the four quantum numbers? Give the quantum numbers for the 19th electron in potassium (K).
4 marks
▾

Four quantum numbers: n (principal), l (azimuthal), ml (magnetic), ms (spin).

K (Z=19): [Ar] 4s¹. The 19th electron is in 4s¹.
n = 4, l = 0, ml = 0, ms = +½


Chapter 3
Why does ionization energy of oxygen (O) is less than nitrogen (N), even though O comes after N in the period?
3 marks
▾

Nitrogen has configuration 2p³ — all three 2p orbitals are half-filled (one electron each). This is extra stable.

Oxygen has configuration 2p⁴ — one 2p orbital has a paired electron. This paired electron experiences electron-electron repulsion, making it easier to remove. Hence IE of O < IE of N.

Why is the electron gain enthalpy of chlorine (Cl) more negative than fluorine (F)?
3 marks
▾

F is a very small atom (n=2 shell). When an electron is added to F, it enters the compact 2p subshell, which already has high electron density. This causes strong electron-electron repulsion, reducing the energy released.

Cl has n=3 shell, which is larger. The incoming electron experiences less repulsion in the more diffuse 3p orbital. So more energy is released → electron gain enthalpy of Cl is more negative than F.

Explain the diagonal relationship with examples.
3 marks
▾

Certain elements in period 2 show similarities in properties with elements diagonally below them in period 3. This is called diagonal relationship.

Pairs: Li–Mg, Be–Al, B–Si

Reason: These diagonal pairs have similar charge/radius ratios (ionic potential), leading to similar polarizing power and hence similar chemical behavior.

Example: Both Li and Mg form nitrides directly with N₂; both have similar solubility behavior of their carbonates.
